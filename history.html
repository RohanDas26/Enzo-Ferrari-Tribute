Blood, Sweat, and Speed

The triumphs that built an empire, and the tragedies that defined a man.

The Triumphs

Dominance at Le Mans

Scuderia Ferrari became synonymous with endurance racing supremacy in the 1950s and 1960s. Enzo viewed Le Mans as the ultimate test of his engines' durability. The Prancing Horse secured 9 overall victories at the 24 Hours of Le Mans, cementing its reputation as an unstoppable force in motorsport.

The beautiful and dominant Ferrari 330 P4.

Formula 1 Royalty

Scuderia Ferrari is the oldest and most successful team in Formula 1 history. Enzo's relentless demand for perfection, often pitting his own drivers against each other to extract maximum performance, led to 15 Drivers' Championships and 16 Constructors' Championships.

The iconic 1961 Ferrari 156 "Sharknose".

The Great Loss

Ford vs. Ferrari (1966)

In 1963, Enzo Ferrari abruptly backed out of a deal to sell his company to Ford Motor Company, angering Henry Ford II. In retaliation, Ford dedicated massive resources to crush Ferrari at Le Mans. The resulting Ford GT40 dealt Enzo a bitter defeat in 1966, breaking Ferrari's winning streak with a famous 1-2-3 finish.

The Tragedies

Motorsport in the mid-20th century was exceptionally dangerous. Enzo Ferrari endured immense personal and public grief, bearing the weight of those who died driving his machines.

Alfredo "Dino" Ferrari (1956)

The deepest wound of Enzo's life was the death of his beloved son and heir apparent, Dino, who died from muscular dystrophy at just 24. A devastated Enzo wore dark sunglasses almost exclusively from that day forward in mourning. He later honored his son by branding a line of V6-powered sports cars "Dino".

1957 Mille Miglia Crash

During the brutal 1,000-mile road race, Alfonso de Portago's Ferrari 335 S suffered a blown tire at 150 mph near Guidizzolo. The horrific crash killed de Portago, his co-driver, and nine spectators (including five children). Enzo was charged with manslaughter, though later acquitted. The tragedy permanently ended the Mille Miglia.

Gilles Villeneuve (1982)

Enzo famously kept his drivers at arm's length emotionally, but he viewed the fearless Canadian driver Gilles Villeneuve almost like a second son. When Villeneuve was killed during qualifying for the 1982 Belgian Grand Prix, it shook Enzo profoundly, marking one of his darkest days in Formula 1.
